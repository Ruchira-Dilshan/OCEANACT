Ocean Pollution

Recognizing the crisis beneath the waves and taking action to address it

Jump to: Key Stats Causes Effects on Marine Life What You Can Do

An estimated 2.2 million species are found in our oceans, which make up more than 70% of the planet's surface. However, they now confront a growing crisis: these enormous ecosystems are being poisoned by pollution from human activities at a startling rate. In line with UN SDG 14: Life Below Water, this article examines the origins, effects, and remedies of ocean pollution.

Global Stats & Facts

Ocean pollution is not a far-off issue; rather, it is a quantifiable, established concern that has an impact on every individual on the planet. These are some of the most remarkable statistics from environmental organizations and current international studies.

8M+ Tonnes of plastic enter the ocean every single year
80% Of ocean pollution originates from land-based sources
700+ Marine species impacted by plastic debris globally
50% Of coral reefs have been lost in the last 30 years
500+ Ocean "dead zones" identified worldwide
$13B Estimated annual economic damage from marine plastic pollution
"By 2050, there could be more plastic in the ocean by weight than fish - unless we act now." - Ellen MacArthur Foundation

Causes of Ocean Pollution

Ocean pollution is caused by numerous interrelated human activities both on land and at sea, rather than having a single source. The first step in treating these factors is to comprehend them.

Plastic Waste

Bottles, bags, straws, and packaging are examples of single-use plastics that are manufactured in massive numbers worldwide. A large portion of this material is disposed of incorrectly and eventually ends up in rivers and the ocean. Instead of completely biodegrading, plastics break down into microplastics, which are minuscule pieces smaller than 5 mm that linger in the environment for hundreds of years and are consumed by marine life at every stage of the food chain.

Industrial Discharge

Untreated or partially treated effluent is frequently released into rivers and coastal areas by factories and industrial units. In addition to chemical solvents and other pollutants that build up in marine sediments and organisms, this wastewater may contain hazardous heavy metals like mercury, lead, and cadmium.

Agricultural Runoff

Rainfall carries pesticides and fertilizers used in farming into rivers and eventually the ocean. Eutrophication is the term for algal blooms brought on by an overabundance of nutrients, especially nitrogen and phosphorus. The majority of marine species cannot survive in hypoxic zones created by these blooms, which reduce the amount of oxygen in the water.

Oil Spills & Shipping

Although major oil spills garner a lot of media attention, regular shipping activities, such as the release of ballast water, fuel leaks, and cargo spills, contribute a significant and continuous amount of hydrocarbon pollution to the oceans. Oil suffocates coastal environments, covers seabird feathers, and jams fish gills.

Urban Sewage & Litter

In many parts of the world, untreated or partially treated sewage finds its way into the ocean, bringing with it extra nutrients, medicines, and diseases. Rain and wind carry urban garbage from parks, pavements, and storm drains straight into the ocean.

Effects on Marine Life

Ocean pollution has an impact on human health, food security, and livelihoods at every level of marine ecosystems, from microscopic plankton to the largest whales.

Entanglement and Ingestion

Marine creatures, such as seabirds, dolphins, seals, and sea turtles, may entangle themselves in abandoned fishing gear (often called "ghost nets") or consume plastic waste that they mistake for prey. Internal cuts, hunger, and digestive system blockage are all caused by ingested plastic. Over 90% of seabird species are thought to have consumed plastic at some time in their lives, according to studies.

Coral Reef Degradation

Despite making up less than 1% of the ocean floor, coral reefs, often referred to as the "rainforests of the sea", support about 25% of all marine species. When paired with ocean warming, pollution impairs coral immunity, encourages illness, and causes coral bleaching episodes. The entire ecosystem that a reef supports collapses as it bleaches and dies.

Bioaccumulation of Toxins

Plankton and tiny fish carry heavy metals and persistent organic pollutants (POPs) from industrial discharge into the food chain. These toxins build up at higher concentrations as larger animals eat smaller ones; this process is known as biomagnification. Humans who regularly eat seafood are at great risk for health problems due to the extremely high amounts of mercury and PCBs found in apex predators like tuna, sharks, and orcas.

Ocean Dead Zones

Algal blooms are fueled by nitrogen overloads caused by agricultural runoff. Large volumes of dissolved oxygen are used by the microorganisms that break down these dead algae, resulting in hypoxic dead zones where practically no marine life can live. The Mississippi River's discharge feeds the Gulf of Mexico dead zone, which can grow to the size of New Jersey during the summer.

What Individuals Can Do

Every level of action is needed to combat ocean pollution, from international policy to personal everyday decisions. We can all take significant, doable action now as citizens and students.

  • Discard single-use plastics. Bring a coffee cup, water bottle, and reusable bag. Refrain from using plastic cutlery and straws. If these tiny changes are broadly implemented, millions of tonnes of plastic might be kept out of the ocean.
  • Participate in or plan a beach clean-up. Work with nearby environmental organizations to clear litter from beaches and waterways. Hundreds of kg of rubbish can be collected in a single event with a small crew.
  • Choose sustainable seafood. Look for Marine Stewardship Council (MSC) certification when shopping. Consumer demand directly influences fishing and aquaculture practices worldwide.
  • Recycle properly. When plastics are recycled improperly, they wind up in landfills and, frequently, the ocean. Sort waste carefully, especially plastics, and be aware of your local recycling regulations.
  • Never dump unwanted medications down the toilet in order to properly dispose of them. Fish hormones and behavior have been shown to be impacted by pharmaceuticals in wastewater. Give any leftover medication back to the pharmacy.
  • Spread trustworthy information on ocean pollution to advocate and raise awareness. Sign petitions in favor of stricter environmental laws. Systemic change is produced by political pressure and cannot be accomplished by individual activity alone.
  • Use eco-friendly products. Choose biodegradable soaps, reef-safe sunscreen, and phosphate-free detergents to prevent dangerous chemicals entering waterways from everyday household use.
"The ocean is not a rubbish bin. Every piece of plastic you refuse, reuse, or recycle is a vote for the world you want to live in."
